The cleanest way in is through an analogy. Think of $h(x,y)$ as the elevation of a landscape. Then the equation $h(x,y) = 0$ carves out a _contour line_ — the set of all locations where the elevation is exactly zero, like the shoreline of a flooded valley. A hiker walking along this shoreline stays at constant elevation by construction: their height never changes as they move.

Three pieces of the picture are worth labeling. First, $h = 0$ is just one specific level set of $h$; there are infinitely many others, one for each constant $c$. Right next to our shoreline are slightly higher and slightly lower contours — $h = +1$, $h = -1$, and so on. Second, the gradient $\nabla h$ at any point is, by definition, the direction in which $h$ increases the fastest. So at any point on the shoreline, $\nabla h$ must point across the contour lines, away from lower values toward higher ones. Third, if we parametrize the hiker's walk by a curve $\gamma(t)$, the velocity $\gamma'(t)$ at any instant is a tangent vector to the curve — it's the direction they're heading at that moment.

Here is what those three objects look like together:

The middle blue curve is the constraint $h=0$. The dashed curves on either side are nearby level sets where $h$ takes the values $-1$ and $+1$. At the point $P$, the orange arrow is $\nabla h(P)$ — it cuts straight across the contours, from the lower value on the left toward the higher value on the right. The green arrow is $\gamma'(0)$, the velocity of an imaginary curve $\gamma(t)$ that an ant walks along the constraint, with the ant sitting at $P$ when $t = 0$. The two arrows meet at a right angle, and the claim is that this is not a coincidence — it has to happen.

Now the proof. Let $\gamma(t)$ be any smooth curve lying entirely on the constraint set, with $\gamma(0) = P$. Since $\gamma(t)$ stays on $h = 0$ for every $t$, we have the identity $$h(\gamma(t)) = 0 \quad \text{for all } t.$$ The left side is a composition of $h$ with $\gamma$, so we can differentiate it using the multivariable chain rule, which says $$\frac{d}{dt}, h(\gamma(t)) ;=; \nabla h(\gamma(t)) \cdot \gamma'(t).$$ The right side of our identity is the constant $0$, whose derivative is $0$. Evaluating at $t = 0$, we get $$\nabla h(P) \cdot \gamma'(0) ;=; 0.$$ A dot product equal to zero is precisely the definition of two vectors being perpendicular. Since $\gamma$ was an arbitrary smooth curve through $P$ on the level set, the vector $\gamma'(0)$ can be _any_ tangent direction at $P$. Therefore $\nabla h(P)$ is perpendicular to every tangent vector at $P$ — equivalently, perpendicular to the whole tangent line (or tangent plane, in higher dimensions) of the constraint at $P$.

Notice how the calculation and the intuition agree perfectly. The gradient points in the direction along which $h$ changes fastest. Walking along $h = 0$, you don't change $h$ at all. So whichever direction you're walking, the gradient must have no component along that direction, which is the geometric meaning of perpendicularity. The dot product being zero in the chain rule equation is just this fact in algebraic clothing.

The same argument generalizes immediately. If $h : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and the constraint $h(x) = 0$ defines a surface or hypersurface in $\mathbb{R}^n$, then for any point $P$ on it, $\nabla h(P)$ is perpendicular to every tangent vector at $P$, hence normal to the entire tangent plane. This is the geometric fact at the heart of Lagrange multipliers: at a constrained extremum of $f$ subject to $h = 0$, the gradient $\nabla f$ has no component along the constraint surface, which forces $\nabla f$ to be parallel to $\nabla h$ — and that's where the equation $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla h$ comes from.
