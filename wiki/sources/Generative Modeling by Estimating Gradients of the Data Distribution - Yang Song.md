# Generative Modeling by Estimating Gradients of the Data Distribution — Yang Song

> Yang Song's companion blog for the score-based generative modeling line. Where [[What are Diffusion Models - Lilian Weng|Lilian Weng's post]] starts from DDPM and bridges *out* to score-matching, this one starts from the **score function** $\nabla_x \log p(x)$ and builds *toward* the same model family — Langevin dynamics for sampling, noise-conditional score networks (NCSN) to fix score-estimation in low-density regions, then the SDE generalization (VE / VP / sub-VP), probability-flow ODE for exact likelihoods, and Bayes-on-scores for inverse problems. Closes by explicitly framing DDPM and score-based modeling as "two formulations of the same family, like wave mechanics and matrix mechanics."

## Summary

The blog is structured around one organizing claim: **modeling the score function $\nabla_x \log p(x)$ sidesteps the intractable normalizing constant $Z_\theta$ that hobbles likelihood-based generative modeling**. With that move, the score-based model $s_\theta(x) \approx \nabla_x \log p(x)$ can have any architecture (no causal masks, no invertibility, no adversarial discriminator), is trained by minimizing the Fisher divergence via score matching, and is sampled by Langevin dynamics $x_{i+1} \leftarrow x_i + \epsilon\,s_\theta(x_i) + \sqrt{2\epsilon}\,z_i$. The naive version fails in practice because score estimates are inaccurate in low-density regions and Langevin samplers initialize there — Song & Ermon's 2019 fix is **multiple noise scales**: perturb data with $\sigma_1 < \cdots < \sigma_L$, train a *noise-conditional* score network $s_\theta(x, i)$ jointly on all of them, then sample with **annealed Langevin dynamics** (Langevin at $\sigma_L$ → at $\sigma_{L-1}$ → … → at $\sigma_1$). Generalizing $L \to \infty$ gives the **SDE formulation** (Song et al. ICLR 2021): forward perturbation is $d\mathbf{x} = f(x,t)\,dt + g(t)\,dw$, reverse is the analytically-known reverse-time SDE $d\mathbf{x} = [f(x,t) - g^2(t)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\,dt + g(t)\,d\bar w$, and a **time-dependent score network** $s_\theta(x, t)$ trained against a continuous Fisher-divergence objective replaces $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ at inference. Three pre-baked SDEs (VE, VP, sub-VP) and the **Predictor-Corrector sampler** (numerical SDE solver + MCMC corrector) give the practical knobs. The same SDE has a **probability-flow ODE** $d\mathbf{x} = [f(x,t) - \tfrac12 g^2(t)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\,dt$ with the same marginals — *deterministic*, *invertible*, and (via the instantaneous-change-of-variables formula) enabling **exact log-likelihood computation**, beating Glow / Flow++ on CIFAR-10 NLL without maximum-likelihood training. Inverse problems get the same treatment: Bayes on scores ($\nabla \log p(x \mid y) = \nabla \log p(x) + \nabla \log p(y \mid x)$) lets a pre-trained unconditional score network solve image inpainting, colorization, MRI reconstruction, and sparse-view CT *without retraining*. The piece ends with an honest historical note: Song discovered the multi-noise-scale trick independently of Sohl-Dickstein 2015 and Ho 2020, and only later (with the ICLR 2021 SDE paper) realized DDPM's ELBO is the same weighted Fisher-divergence objective in disguise.

For this vault, Song's blog is the **counterpart perspective** to Weng's, and it is the one that makes the score-additivity identity (the engine behind [[Compositional Diffusion Constraint Solvers]]) feel *foundational* rather than incidental — Song's whole framing is "the score is the thing; the rest is sampling machinery."

## Key claims

- **The intractable $Z_\theta$ goes away when you model the score.** For any $p_\theta(x) = e^{-f_\theta(x)}/Z_\theta$, $\nabla_x \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_x f_\theta(x)$ — the normalizing constant differentiates to zero. So the score-based model "can be parameterized without worrying about the normalizing constant" and architecture choice is unconstrained.
- **Score matching minimizes the Fisher divergence without knowing the data score.** Three estimators in common use — **denoising score matching** (Vincent 2011), **sliced score matching** (Song et al. 2019, random projections), and **explicit score matching** with integration-by-parts (Hyvärinen 2005). All optimize $\mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\|\nabla_x \log p(x) - s_\theta(x)\|^2$ without access to the data score.
- **Naive score + Langevin fails because of low-density regions.** The Fisher divergence weights errors by $p(x)$, so low-density regions are silently ignored — but that's exactly where a Langevin chain initialized from noise has to start. *"Having an inaccurate score-based model will derail Langevin dynamics from the very beginning."*
- **Multi-noise perturbation populates low-density regions.** Larger $\sigma$ smooths $p(x) * \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$ so the perturbed density has support everywhere; train one noise-conditional model $s_\theta(x, i)$ to jointly estimate scores at all noise levels $\sigma_1 < \cdots < \sigma_L$ using the weighted Fisher loss $\sum_i \lambda(i)\,\mathbb{E}_{p_{\sigma_i}(x)}\|\nabla \log p_{\sigma_i}(x) - s_\theta(x, i)\|^2$ with $\lambda(i) = \sigma_i^2$.
- **Annealed Langevin sampling is the discrete-time recipe.** Run Langevin dynamics at $\sigma_L$ → $\sigma_{L-1}$ → … → $\sigma_1$, decreasing noise scale at each stage. NCSN/NCSNv2 with this sampler beat SOTA Inception score on CIFAR-10 at the time.
- **Practical recipe (Song & Ermon 2020): geometric $\sigma$ schedule + U-Net + EMA weights at test time.** $L$ on the order of hundreds to thousands; $\sigma_1$ small, $\sigma_L \approx$ max pairwise data distance.
- **SDE generalization sends $L \to \infty$.** Forward perturbation $d\mathbf{x} = f(x, t)\,dt + g(t)\,d\mathbf{w}$ replaces the discrete schedule. Three useful SDEs for images: **VE** (variance exploding — generalizes NCSN), **VP** (variance preserving — generalizes DDPM), **sub-VP** (interpolates between them). The SDE choice is part of the model, not a fixed assumption.
- **The reverse-time SDE has a closed form, parameterized by the score.** Anderson 1982: $d\mathbf{x} = [f(x,t) - g^2(t)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\,dt + g(t)\,d\bar w$. Substitute the trained $s_\theta(x, t)$ for the score and the reverse process is fully specified.
- **Training objective is continuous Fisher-divergence.** $\mathbb{E}_{t \sim \mathcal{U}(0,T)}\,\mathbb{E}_{p_t(x)}[\lambda(t)\|\nabla_x \log p_t(x) - s_\theta(x, t)\|^2]$. The **likelihood weighting** $\lambda(t) = g^2(t)$ makes the objective an upper bound on the KL divergence (and hence on negative log-likelihood) up to a $\mathrm{KL}(p_T \| \pi)$ constant — connecting score-based training to maximum likelihood.
- **Numerical SDE solvers: Euler-Maruyama, Milstein, stochastic Runge-Kutta, reverse-diffusion (their own variant), and adaptive-step-size.** Euler-Maruyama is qualitatively the same as Langevin: $\Delta\mathbf{x} = [f(x,t) - g^2(t)s_\theta(x,t)]\Delta t + g(t)\sqrt{|\Delta t|}\,z$.
- **Predictor-Corrector samplers exploit a structural property.** Since they only need to sample from each *marginal* $p_t(x)$ and not follow a specific trajectory, an MCMC corrector (Langevin / HMC) can be applied after each predictor (SDE-solver) step to clean up samples at fixed $t$. This is the sampler that posted state-of-the-art FID 2.20 on CIFAR-10, beating StyleGAN2+ADA's 2.92.
- **Probability-flow ODE: deterministic, invertible, exact likelihoods.** Every SDE has a corresponding ODE $d\mathbf{x} = [f(x,t) - \tfrac12 g^2(t)\nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\,dt$ with the same marginals. Plugging in $s_\theta$ makes it a *neural ODE* / continuous normalizing flow → instantaneous change of variables gives exact log-likelihood. Achieves SOTA 2.99 bits/dim on CIFAR-10 without any maximum-likelihood training; with likelihood-weighted training, comparable to autoregressive Image Transformer (2.83 vs 2.80 bits/dim).
- **Bayes on scores solves inverse problems with a pre-trained unconditional model.** Take the gradient of $\log p(x \mid y) \propto \log p(x) + \log p(y \mid x)$ on both sides: $\nabla_x \log p(x \mid y) = \nabla_x \log p(x) + \nabla_x \log p(y \mid x)$. If $s_\theta(x) \approx \nabla_x \log p(x)$ is pre-trained, and the forward likelihood $p(y \mid x)$ is known (forward operator + noise model), the posterior score is computable for free. Demonstrated on inpainting, colorization, accelerated MRI, sparse-view CT — *without retraining the score model per task*.
- **NCSN and DDPM are two formulations of the same family.** Sohl-Dickstein 2015 introduced discrete diffusion probabilistic models; Song & Ermon 2019 independently developed NCSN from score matching. Ho et al. 2020 first noticed ELBO ≡ weighted score-matching. Song et al. ICLR 2021 made the unification formal via the SDE framing. *"Both are different perspectives of the same model family, much like wave mechanics and matrix mechanics in quantum mechanics."*
- **Two open challenges remain (as of the post's last revision).** (i) Slow sampling — many Langevin or SDE-solver iterations per sample; addressable by ODE solvers, distillation, and direct latent-to-image maps but at quality cost. (ii) Discrete data — scores are only defined on continuous distributions; workarounds are learn-an-autoencoder-then-diffuse-on-the-latent (latent diffusion / [Symbolic Music Generation with Diffusion Models]) or Sohl-Dickstein's original discrete diffusion.

## Notable quotes

- *"The key idea is to model the gradient of the log probability density function, a quantity often known as the (Stein) score function."* — Song's framing of the whole research line in one sentence. The rest of the post is "and here is what falls out." (Introduction.)
- *"The key challenge is the fact that the estimated score functions are inaccurate in low density regions, where few data points are available for computing the score matching objective."* — the empirical observation that motivated the multi-noise-scale trick; the entire NCSN → SDE arc is a response to this. (Naive score-based generative modeling and its pitfalls.)
- *"By generalizing the number of noise scales to infinity, we obtain not only higher quality samples, but also, among others, exact log-likelihood computation, and controllable generation for inverse problem solving."* — the unification claim that justifies the rest of the post. (SDEs section opening.)
- *"Both score-based generative modeling with multiple noise perturbations and diffusion probabilistic models are different perspectives of the same model family, much like how wave mechanics and matrix mechanics are equivalent formulations of quantum mechanics in the history of physics."* — the explicit equivalence statement that resolves the Weng-vs-Song framing tension. (Connection to diffusion models and others.)
- *"The perspective of score matching and score-based models allows one to calculate log-likelihoods exactly, solve inverse problems naturally, and is directly connected to energy-based models, Schrödinger bridges and optimal transport."* — the case for why both viewpoints are worth holding. (Same section.)

## Open questions

- **For Diffusion Policy, which SDE family (VE / VP / sub-VP) does Chi et al. 2023 actually use?** Default DDPM is VP; the paper may use a different schedule. Resolve on deep-read.
- **Does the probability-flow ODE perspective buy anything in robotics?** Exact likelihood evaluation could enable detecting out-of-distribution observations during deployment ("is this scene something the policy has seen?"). Worth investigating once a policy is trained.
- **Inverse-problem framing for robot manipulation.** Bayes-on-scores naturally fits "given a desired effect $y$, sample an action $x$ that achieves it." This is exactly what [[Compositional Diffusion Constraint Solvers]] does for spatial arrangement — is there a more direct analog for closed-loop control? Open question for the [[Diffusion Policy]] deep-read.
- **Discrete data handling.** Song flags discrete data as an open problem in 2021. By 2026 there are mature approaches (D3PM, discrete-flow matching, masked diffusion). Worth a future synthesis if discrete actions / discrete planning ever become a thread.
